Principles of Modulation

Why is Modulation Needed?
1. **Antenna Size Reduction**: The size of a dipole antenna is proportional to wavelength (λ = c/f). Without modulation, transmitting audio frequencies (e.g. 3 kHz) requires antennas over 50 km long! Modulation shifts the signal to MHz bands, reducing antenna size to meters.
2. **Multiplexing**: Multiple transmitters can transmit over the same channel simultaneously on separate frequencies without interfering.
3. **Noise Immunity**: Certain modulation types (like FM) exchange bandwidth for high noise immunity.

Vestigial Sideband (VSB) Principles

VSB is a compromise between SSB and DSB. Filtering out a sideband completely (SSB) requires an ideal brickwall filter, which is impossible to implement practically. VSB solves this by passing one complete sideband and a **vestige** (small portion, typically 25%) of the other sideband.

Filter Method

Phase Shift Method Derivation

The Hilbert transform shifts all frequency components of the message signal by -90°: $\hat{m}(t) = A_m \sin(2\pi f_m t)$.
By combining in phase and quadrature paths:

s(t) = m(t)\cos(2\pi f_c t) \mp \hat{m}(t)\sin(2\pi f_c t)

Subtracting (-) selects the Upper Sideband (USB); adding (+) selects the Lower Sideband (LSB).

Bessel Sideband Coefficients

Modulation Index (β) 2.4

As β increases, the power spreads into higher-order sideband components ($J_1, J_2, J_3$). At specific indices (e.g. β = 2.4, 5.5), the carrier amplitude $J_0(β)$ goes to zero! This is the carrier-null calibration method.

Time Division Multiplexing

Commutator Sampling Rate 1000 Hz

TDM samples multiple signals sequentially in time. A distributor switch (commutator) rotates at the sampling rate, allocating a specific time slice to each channel within a frame. Look at Channel 2 on the oscilloscope to see the PAM interleaved frames!
